EDUCATION ALERT

Nation’s Children Now Required to Declare Identity Before Learning to Read

Officials say early self-classification improves efficiency, reduces confusion, and ensures no child accidentally develops an unauthorized sense of self.

By Staff Visionary, One True Infotainment

NEW CHICAGO — Before opening their books Monday morning, millions of American schoolchildren placed their hands over their hearts, faced the SmartBoard, and selected who they were allowed to be that day.

The new Identity First Learning Initiative, rolled out nationwide overnight, requires students as young as four to declare their full ideological, emotional, and existential profile before receiving instruction in basic subjects like reading, math, and sanctioned breathing.

“Children can’t learn if they don’t know what they are,” said Deputy Undersecretary for Developmental Alignment Karen Phelps, watching a kindergartener hesitate between “Future Leader” and “Potential Disruption Risk.” “Once we lock that in, everything else becomes much simpler.”

Teachers report that instruction time has been reduced by nearly 80 percent, freeing up valuable hours for reassessment, correction, and emotional recalibration drills. In one second-grade classroom, a boy formerly identified as “Curious” was successfully reclassified as “Pre-Noncompliant” after asking why the sun exists.

Parents across the country have expressed relief.

“I used to worry about my daughter falling behind in reading,” said one mother, scrolling through her child’s daily Identity Report Card. “Now I know exactly who she is, what she thinks, and when that changes. That’s real education.”

Officials insist the program is not about control, but clarity.

“Freedom is confusing,” Phelps explained. “Children deserve structure. Boundaries. Approved options.”

At press time, the Department of Educational Stability confirmed Phase Two of the rollout would begin next week, allowing schools to begin assigning identities proactively for students who struggle to choose correctly on their own.
