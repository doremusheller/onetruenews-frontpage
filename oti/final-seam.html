Grundy Praises the Wall’s Final Seam; Banners Hung, Nation Unified in View

With bright cloth and brighter conviction, the Corrected State completes its border beautification program. The First Citizen calls it “a flawless alignment of policy and panorama.”

Under halogen daylight and televised optimism, a diverse team of national workers gathered at the wall’s final breach this morning. They arrived with rolls of red and indigo fabric, emblazoned with the symbols of ongoing success. At exactly 0900, on cue with the morning anthem, the First Citizen appeared on a mobile dais to oversee the ritual of closure.

“No wall is ever finished,” Grundy announced, “until the people’s reflection decorates its surface.” Behind him, technicians unrolled a banner depicting the national crest surrounded by smiling silhouettes of every profession, from engineer to barista. Each figure waved at the same horizon, as prescribed by the Bureau of Unity Optics.

The workers—selected from all districts and professions—performed the hanging sequence with ceremonial precision. A state engineer described the maneuver as “a ballet of bolts and belief.” Reporters noted that each banner aligned seamlessly with the next, obscuring what officials described as “temporary breathing gaps.” The result resembled a continuous fabric horizon: a triumph of textile diplomacy.

At midpoint, Grundy applauded and presented the crew with the Medal of Structural Sincerity, pinned onto their safety harnesses while the crowd chanted the civic oath: “Measure Twice, Believe Once.” Drone cameras broadcast the event nationwide, the footage already pre-approved for textbook inclusion.

The Ministry of Borders and Optics issued a statement confirming that “every visible seam now serves an aesthetic purpose.” Citizens were encouraged to visit designated observation decks to “witness continuity made visible.” A pilot program will allow families to sponsor individual bolts, engraved with the phrase “This Fastener Brought to You by Stability.”

By evening, the floodlights dimmed to a soft amber, and the banners fluttered like synchronized applause. Grundy delivered his closing words in front of the final panel: “Some nations build walls to divide; we build ours to display understanding.” The audience rose on cue, waving flags that exactly matched the ones printed on the wall behind them.

As the anthem reprise echoed, the workers filed out through illuminated arches marked EXIT / ENTRY (AS NEEDED). The cameras lingered on the seamless expanse, reflecting the First Citizen’s grin multiplied a thousandfold. Broadcasters concluded the coverage with a standard assurance: “The border remains perfect and porous in equal measure—by design.”
